Why this tool exists

Hospitals across the U.S. are under sustained financial pressure from declining reimbursement, rising operating costs, and reductions in public funding. Because labor is typically a hospital's largest controllable operating expense, workforce reductions — hiring freezes, layoffs, furloughs, service cuts — often become the default response when revenue declines. Those reductions fall hardest on the people who deliver care.

This calculator explores one alternative. The goal is not the savings — it's preserving jobs, care teams, and institutional knowledge. Savings are simply the mechanism. It models whether many people making small, voluntary, transparent contributions could reduce the need to eliminate positions.

The calculator is intentionally designed to evaluate proportional burden-sharing across income levels. Every scenario includes equity measures, so the distribution of contributions is visible rather than assumed.

It advocates for no specific program. Every modeled dollar should be weighed against the human cost of workforce reductions — this calculator exists to make those tradeoffs transparent, not to decide them.

Why Salaried Earners Work Differently
Salaried exempt employees are paid a fixed amount regardless of hours worked. Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), docking pay for partial-day absences is generally not permitted without risking loss of exempt status — so "early clock-out" has no automatic payroll effect for this group.

Two mechanisms may work for salaried staff:
PTO / Vacation Donation
Employees elect to forfeit a set number of PTO hours per year. PTO is a real balance-sheet liability — donating it reduces what the hospital owes. Cleanest option, no FLSA risk.
Voluntary Salary Reduction
A formal, temporary reduction in base pay (e.g. 1–2%). Most impactful financially. Requires a written agreement and may have state-level notice requirements. Symbolically powerful when leadership leads.

Core Formula
Per Employee / Year =
  (Minutes ÷ 60) × Hourly Wage × (Shifts/Wk × Weeks/Yr)

Total Savings =
  Per-Employee Savings × (# Employees × Participation %)
Flat minutes: same reduction for all employees — simplest to administer.
Tiered by pay band: fewer minutes for lower wages — protects proportional take-home pay.
Percentage of hours: e.g. 1% of annual paid hours — scales automatically with compensation.
PTO donation: employees elect to forfeit PTO hours — no effect on daily scheduling.

Remote Workers — Quick Note
Remote workers use the same formula as on-site staff and typically follow a 5×8 or 4×10 pattern. Their schedule flexibility and software-based time tracking make them a practical pilot group — early clock-out has no patient care impact.
⚠ Timekeeping caveat: If the system auto-fills early clock-out minutes from PTO/APL banks (e.g. Symplr), use a dedicated $0 pay code or the PTO donation model instead to avoid unintended leave drawdown.
Higher Earners — Why the Math Is Different
Early clock-out does not apply to salaried exempt employees. The standard minutes-per-shift formula cannot be used for this group. Under the FLSA, salaried exempt employees must receive their full salary in any week they perform work — docking pay for partial-day absences generally results in loss of exempt status, creating significant legal and payroll risk.

Two alternative mechanisms produce real savings for this group:

Pay equity signal: Without proportional participation across pay levels, lower-paid employees would bear a relatively larger share of the total contribution effort. In this example, a director contributing $1,450–$2,900 annually compared to a CNA contributing approximately $875 (based on 10 minutes per shift) illustrates how absolute contributions scale differently. The tiered model is designed to align participation more closely with compensation to support overall fairness.
